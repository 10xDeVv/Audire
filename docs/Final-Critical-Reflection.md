# Audire: Final Critical Reflection

**Body word count: 375**

Audire is valuable because self-taught musicians often have musical intuition without regular access to a teacher who can name, question, and develop what they play. As a self-taught gospel pianist, I wanted AI to provide that missing conversation without becoming an authority on what “good” music should sound like. A musician enters a short progression, key, style, and goal; Audire returns a harmonic reading, three creative paths, playable comparisons, and a practice plan. The musician can preserve, develop, reject, or rewrite every suggestion.

Adam Patrick Bell’s “Pedagogy of the Prompt” helped me see that an AI interface is never neutral. Bell argues that systems such as MusicFX DJ, Music AI Sandbox, and Udio can make generation appear magical while concealing the algorithms, datasets, business interests, and educational values connecting an input to an output. Audire responds by showing its evidence, assumptions, unknowns, and confidence. It does not claim to hear voicing, rhythm, touch, or intention when it has only received chord symbols.

Taylor Brook’s “Music, Art, Machine Learning, and Standardization” sharpened a second design problem: models can reproduce cultural norms, yet artists can also use machine learning to resist standardization and explore their medium. Brook discusses Annie Dorsen’s algorithmic theatre as an artistic practice that exposes computation instead of hiding it. Similarly, Audire offers Preserve, Develop, and Explore paths with explicit tradeoffs rather than one optimized answer. Playback makes the musician’s ear, not the model’s probability, the final judge.

The prototype also revealed AI’s limits through failure. An early response called `Dm7 - Gm7` a ii-V progression, confusing a minor seventh chord with a dominant seventh and inventing a key. I revised the prompt, added an optional tonal centre, made uncertainty visible, and built theory evaluations covering ambiguous harmony, cadences, slash chords, and gospel secondary dominants. This does not eliminate error; it makes error part of the tool’s critical design.

Ethically, Audire may still favour common Western chord labels and genre stereotypes, especially when describing gospel, jazz, or R&B traditions represented unevenly in training data. It also depends on a commercial AI provider. Its accessibility is therefore real but conditional. Audire’s strongest role is not generating finished art. It is helping musicians compare possibilities, practise intentionally, and keep authorship over the musical decisions that matter.
